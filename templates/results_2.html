Gene names, accession numbers, and database links

FOXJ1 goes by several other names in older literature and databases, including HFH4, HFH-4, CILD43, and FKHL13. FOXJ1 has exactly two transcripts. The first is NM_001454.4, which is the main reviewed transcript and carries the MANE Select label. The second is XM_047435666.1, a computer-predicted version. Both produce a protein of 421 amino acids. We studied both transcripts in depth and compare them in the tables below.

What this means

The gene

FOXJ1 sits on chromosome 17 and spans about 4.9 kb of DNA on the minus strand. The minus strand orientation means the 5' end of the RNA points toward higher chromosome coordinate numbers, and the 3' end with the poly(A) tail points toward lower numbers. The gene has three exons and two introns in its main transcript. The promoter region has multiple regulatory elements upstream of the transcription start site, including TATA boxes, GC boxes, CCAAT boxes, and initiator elements, all identified from the EPD database [11]. These elements help control when and in which cells the gene is switched on.

Why we chose these two transcripts

We chose NM_001454.4 as our main reference because it is the MANE Select transcript. MANE stands for Matched Annotation from NCBI and EMBL-EBI, and the Select label means it is the single best-supported transcript for this gene, chosen based on experimental data [19]. It produces a 421 amino acid protein (NP_001445.2), which is the protein we use throughout the rest of this project.

We included XM_047435666.1 as a comparison because it is a computer-predicted version of the same gene. It produces the same 421 amino acid protein but predicts a longer 5' UTR (850 bp instead of 301 bp) and a simpler exon structure with only two exons and one intron. Comparing the two shows how prediction tools can give a different picture of the non-coding parts of a gene even when the protein sequence is the same.

How the two transcripts differ in their splicing

Both transcripts share the same last exon. In NM_001454.4 the 5' region is split into exon 1, intron 1, then exon 2 before reaching the shared last exon. In XM_047435666.1 the whole 5' region is predicted as a single large first exon, so the splice map looks different even though the coding blocks are identical and both produce the same protein.

A longer 5' UTR like the one in XM_047435666.1 could in theory affect how much protein is made or how stable the mRNA is, because the 5' UTR can contain regulatory sequences that affect translation [14]. Without experimental data we cannot confirm whether this predicted isoform exists in cells or whether the longer UTR changes anything in practice.

Gene expression

FOXJ1 is expressed in a very specific set of tissues, all of which carry motile cilia. The strongest expression is in the bronchial mucosa of the lung, the endometrium, and the fallopian tube. Medium expression is seen in the nasopharynx, efferent ducts, and choroid plexus. Low expression is detected in the testis and cerebral cortex. Essentially no FOXJ1 protein is found in tissues like liver, skeletal muscle, skin, bone marrow, spleen, or lymph node, all of which lack motile cilia [22].

This narrow expression pattern makes sense for a transcription factor whose only known job is to switch on the motile cilia formation program. It does not need to be on in cells that do not build cilia.

Which transcript is expressed in which tissue

Because NM_001454.4 is the experimentally supported MANE Select transcript, it is the one we can confidently say is expressed in ciliated tissues. XM_047435666.1 is a computer-predicted isoform and there is no isoform-level RNA-seq data that confirms it is actually produced in specific tissues. Both transcripts encode the same 421 amino acid protein, so at the protein level there is no difference between them. The tissue expression data from the Human Protein Atlas and GTEx reflects the combined signal for FOXJ1 without distinguishing between the two transcripts [22] (GTEx Portal; References — tools).

In terms of physiological state, FOXJ1 is expressed in ciliated cells that are actively maintaining their motile cilia. In the airway epithelium this means mature multiciliated cells responsible for mucociliary clearance. In the fallopian tube it is expressed in the ciliated cells that help move the egg toward the uterus. In the choroid plexus and ependyma it is in the cells whose cilia circulate cerebrospinal fluid through the brain ventricles [22].

For a discussion of how these expression patterns connect to the specific functions of the FOXJ1 protein, see the Homolog Protein Function page.

Development-related differential gene expression

Differential gene expression means a gene is turned on or off at different levels depending on the time, tissue, or stage of development. Not all genes are active all the time in all cells. Some switch on only at specific points in development and then stay on, while others are active only briefly. When a gene follows a consistent pattern of being on in some developmental stages and off in others, we call that development-related differential expression [14].

Which transcripts show development-related differential expression

Both FOXJ1 transcripts, NM_001454.4 and XM_047435666.1, produce the same 421 amino acid protein, so at the protein level they cannot be distinguished. The key difference between them is the 5' UTR: NM_001454.4 has a 301 bp 5' UTR and XM_047435666.1 has an 850 bp 5' UTR. A longer 5' UTR can contain regulatory elements such as upstream open reading frames or RNA secondary structures that affect how much protein gets made from the mRNA [14]. This means it is possible that at certain developmental stages one transcript produces more FOXJ1 protein than the other, even though both carry the same coding sequence. Without isoform-level RNA-seq experiments we cannot confirm this, but it is a real possibility given the size difference in the UTRs.

What we can say with confidence is that FOXJ1 RNA is detected across virtually every stage of human life. The EMBL-EBI Expression Atlas developmental RNA survey for FOXJ1 (ENSG00000129654) shows signal starting at 4 weeks post-conception and continuing consistently through the following prenatal stages: 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 16, 18, and 19 weeks post-conception. After birth, expression continues across every postnatal stage: fetal, neonate, infant, toddler, school-age child, juvenile, adolescent, young adult, middle adult, and elderly (database; see References — tools).

Differences in expression across development

The expression level of FOXJ1 is not the same at every stage. The heatmap in Figure 3 shows that signal is strongest during the prenatal window, particularly between weeks 4 and 13 post-conception, where multiple tissue datasets show high expression. This early prenatal signal is explained by the fact that FOXJ1 is needed very early in embryo development to set the left-right body axis. At around weeks 4 to 6, the embryonic node contains monociliated cells whose beating cilia generate a directional fluid flow that determines which side of the body becomes left and which becomes right. FOXJ1 must be active at this stage for those cilia to form at all [25].

Later in prenatal development, FOXJ1 expression shifts to the developing lung, where it drives airway epithelial progenitor cells to differentiate into mature multiciliated cells. This happens before the structural cilia genes are switched on, which fits with FOXJ1's role as the upstream activator that starts the whole cilia-building process [25]. After birth, expression continues at lower but consistent levels across all tissues that need to maintain their motile cilia throughout life.

How this connects to FOXJ1 function

The developmental expression pattern of FOXJ1 maps directly onto every biological context where motile cilia are needed. It is on early in the embryo when the body axis is being set. It goes up in the developing lung when airway cilia are being built. It stays on in adult ciliated tissues to keep those cilia working. This is exactly what you would expect from a master regulator whose entire job is to activate the motile cilia program [25].

When FOXJ1 is lost or mutated, the consequences match this expression pattern closely. Patients with CILD43 have problems in every tissue where FOXJ1 would normally be expressed: chronic lung disease from failed mucociliary clearance, randomized organ positioning from failed embryonic node cilia, and in some cases hydrocephalus from failed ependymal cilia [25]. This makes FOXJ1 one of the clearest examples of a transcription factor whose development-related expression pattern directly controls organ formation across multiple tissue systems.
