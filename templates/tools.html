Tools & Methods

All the tools, databases, and websites we used throughout this project, from finding the homolog to building this site.

Click + / − to expand each tool card.

Homology, sequences & genome browsing

NCBI BLAST (BLASTp)

Used for: Searching our query protein against the full non-redundant protein database to find the closest known match. This is how we identified FOXJ1 as the human homolog. We also used the built-in distance tree to see how our query groups with other FOXJ1 sequences, and checked the pairwise alignment against human NP_001445.2 to confirm the match position by position.

NCBI Gene — FOXJ1 (2302)

Used for: Looking up the official gene name, synonyms, chromosomal location, and links to RefSeq transcripts. We also used the transcript table here to compare the different mRNA versions of FOXJ1.

NCBI Nucleotide (RefSeq)

Used for: Pulling up the chromosome reference sequence (NC_000017.11), the RefSeqGene record (NG_013345.1), the curated mRNA (NM_001454.4), and a predicted mRNA isoform (XM_047435666.1). We used these to get exon positions, coding sequence coordinates, and poly(A) site information.

NCBI Genome Data Viewer

Used for: Visually confirming where FOXJ1 sits on chromosome 17 and checking the exon-intron layout. We used this to get the genomic coordinates shown in our gene diagrams and tables.

EPD / EPDnew (human promoters)

Used for: Finding candidate promoter elements upstream of the FOXJ1 transcription start site, including TATA boxes, initiator elements, GC boxes, and CCAAT boxes. Each predicted motif comes with a p-value.

Expression & pathways

Human Protein Atlas — FOXJ1

Used for: Checking where FOXJ1 RNA and protein actually show up across human tissues. This confirmed that expression is concentrated in ciliated cell types like airway epithelium, which fits what the protein does.

GTEx Portal — FOXJ1

Used for: A second independent source of tissue RNA expression data for FOXJ1, used alongside the Human Protein Atlas to back up our expression discussion.

Reactome

Used for: Linking FOXJ1 to reviewed biological pathways, particularly those related to cilia formation and the cell cycle. We cited specific Reactome pathway IDs in the protein function section.

Secondary structure & 3D structure

JPred4

Used for: Predicting which parts of the 395-residue FOXJ1 sequence fold into helices, beta strands, or coil regions. The prediction figures and our discussion of them are on the structure results page.

PSIPRED (UCL Protein Analysis Workbench)

Used for: A second independent secondary structure prediction for the same sequence, to cross-check JPred4. Both tools gave the same picture: mostly helical with long disordered regions, which is normal for a transcription factor.

SWISS-MODEL

Used for: Building predicted 3D homology models for FOXJ1. The server returned two models using different templates. Both are clearly labelled as predicted, not experimental. Primary citation: Waterhouse et al., Nucleic Acids Research (2018).

AlphaFold Protein Structure Database (Q92949)

Used for: The full-length predicted structure for human FOXJ1, coloured by confidence score (pLDDT), with a pTM score and a PAE heatmap. Cited per Varadi et al. (Nucleic Acids Research 2022) and Jumper et al. (Nature 2021).

AlphaFold Server (DeepMind)

Used for: Optional—run your own AlphaFold jobs through a browser. We only point to it on the structure page for people who want to try a fresh prediction.

RCSB PDB (wwPDB)

Used for: Downloading experimental structure coordinates, specifically 2C6Y, a crystal structure of a related forkhead protein bound to DNA. This was used as the template for SWISS-MODEL Model 02 and as a reference point in the structure discussion.

3Dmol.js

Used for: Showing interactive 3D views of model_01.pdb and model_02.pdb directly in the browser so readers can rotate and explore the structures without installing anything. Citation: Rego & Koes, Nucleic Acids Research 2022.

Protein annotation & analysis

UniProt — FOXJ1 (Q92949)

Used for: The main protein reference. We used it for function descriptions, subcellular location, post-translational modification notes, and literature links. It is also the accession that connects to the AlphaFold and SWISS-MODEL workflows.

InterPro — FOXJ1 (Q92949)

Used for: Domain and family labels for FOXJ1, alongside CDD. InterPro rolls several signature databases into one page, which saved us from clicking through each source separately.

NCBI Conserved Domain Database (CDD)

Used for: Identifying the FH_FOXJ1 forkhead domain hit and the predicted DNA-binding residue positions. The domain boundaries from CDD were used to draw the domain figure on the structure page. Citation: Lu et al., Nucleic Acids Research 2020.

ExPASy ProtParam

Used for: Rough molecular weight and pI from the sequence—the same ExPASy calculator everyone cites in lab reports.

Website delivery

HTML

Used for: Writing the structure and content of every page, including headings, sections, figures, tables, navigation, and the embedded 3D viewers.

CSS

Used for: All the visual styling, including layout, fonts, colors, the navigation bar, tables, figure captions, and the horizontal tool card scroll sections (static/css/style.css).

JavaScript

Used for: Small interactive features like the mobile navigation toggle, the background animation, scroll-triggered figure hints, and the left and right scroll buttons on the tool card sections (static/js/main.js).

Jinja2 (templates)

Used for: Keeping the site consistent. Each results page extends a shared base template so the header, navigation, and styling are the same across all pages. Individual pages only fill in their own content.

Flask (Python)

Used for: Running the site as a local web server. Flask handles routing between pages, serves the templates, and delivers static files like images and PDB coordinates to the browser.

Methods

We started with a partial amino acid sequence from a purified protein sample. We used public databases and free web tools to figure out the closest human protein, then studied that protein's gene, function, and 3D structure.

Finding the closest homolog (BLASTp)

We ran NCBI BLASTp, which compares a protein sequence against all known proteins in the database. We kept all settings at the NCBI defaults. Our query was 395 amino acids long and had no name attached to it.

Every top result was a FOXJ1 protein from a primate. The best hits had 100% query coverage and E-values of 0.0. We picked the human RefSeq entry NP_001445.2 for follow-up because it is a manually curated record and had about 98% identity to our query.

We also looked at the BLAST distance tree to see how our query sits relative to other FOXJ1 sequences from different species, and we went through the pairwise alignment to the human protein to check the match position by position. That is how we confirmed FOXJ1 as the homolog.

Gene structure, DNA layout, and promoters

To study the gene we used NCBI Gene, the RefSeq nucleotide records for NM_001454.4 and XM_047435666.1, and the Genome Data Viewer to confirm the chromosomal positions and exon layout on the human genome. For promoter elements we used EPDnew, which reports a p-value for each predicted motif. For expression data we used the Human Protein Atlas and GTEx. Where we needed to reference a specific biological pathway we used Reactome.

Protein function and domains

For protein function we used UniProt entry Q92949 as the main reference, with InterPro and the NCBI CDD for domain information. Molecular weight estimates follow the standard ExPASy ProtParam approach.

Structure prediction and viewing

We ran the sequence through JPred4 and PSIPRED for secondary structure prediction and compared both outputs. For 3D models we used SWISS-MODEL and the AlphaFold Database entry for human FOXJ1. We also referenced the experimental forkhead structure 2C6Y from the PDB where it helped the discussion. Domain boundaries in figures come from CDD, InterPro, and UniProt. The interactive 3D viewers on the structure page use 3Dmol.js with PDB files downloaded from SWISS-MODEL.

How this website was built

The site runs on Flask. Pages are written in HTML, styled with CSS, and use a small amount of JavaScript for interactive features. Jinja2 templates keep the layout consistent across all pages without repeating code.
