The Structure of FOXJ1

Secondary structure predictions, homology models, and AlphaFold analysis.

All structure predictions and secondary structure analyses were run on the 395-residue query sequence used in our BLAST analysis. The full-length human FOXJ1 protein is 421 amino acids long (RefSeq NP_001445.2), so there is a small gap at the ends.

Secondary structure predictions

We ran the sequence through JPred4 and PSIPRED. Each method labels positions as helix, beta strand, or coil from the amino acid sequence alone.

JPred4

The full 395-residue sequence was submitted to JPred4 at the University of Dundee. JPred combines several neural network methods to assign each amino acid as helix (H), beta strand (E), or coil, and gives a confidence score for each position.

Figure 1. JPred4 for FOXJ1 (1 to 395): pink = helix, yellow = strand, grey = coil. Mostly helices and loops, little sheet, as expected for a forkhead protein. Source: JPred4.

Figure 1 shows several alpha-helical blocks with coil between them and only short strand segments, consistent with a forkhead-type transcription factor.

PSIPRED

The same sequence was run through PSIPRED at University College London. PSIPRED color-codes each amino acid by its predicted type: pink for helix, yellow for beta strand, and grey for coil. The two figures below come from the same run and both show the same result.

Figure 2. PSIPRED sequence plot (1 to 395); colours as Figure 1. Sparse strands, agrees with JPred4. PSIPRED: this run; Buchan & Jones 2019 [6].
Figure 3. PSIPRED chart for the same submission as Figure 2 (summary of helix/strand/coil propensities). Source: same as Figure 2.

JPred4 and PSIPRED share the same result: FOXJ1 is helix-rich with little beta sheet, in line with the FOX family and with the 3D models below.

Predicted homology models (SWISS-MODEL), PREDICTED

We used SWISS-MODEL for homology models. The server aligns our sequence to templates with known experimental structures and returns 3D coordinates. Output is computational, not a crystal structure.

SWISS-MODEL returned two models. Model 01 is the stronger one and our main 3D reference. Model 02 uses a lower-identity X-ray template and is only for comparison.

Figure 4. SWISS-MODEL summary cards (predicted models, not experimental). Ref: Waterhouse et al., NAR 2018 [26]; project.

Predicted homology model 1

Model 01 was built using an AlphaFold template based on UniProt entry Q92949. The sequence identity between this template and FOXJ1 is 97.97%, which is very high and means the template is closely matched to our protein. The model scored a GMQE of 0.59, which falls within a range generally considered acceptable for comparative modelling. Because of its high sequence identity and reasonable model quality score, Model 01 is used as the main three-dimensional reference for the structural analysis below.

Tertiary structure, what the 3D shape looks like

From Figure 4 and Figure 5, FOXJ1 has a compact helical core and long flexible tails. The folded segment shows up as a dense block in SWISS-MODEL and AlphaFold; the termini stay poorly defined and AlphaFold colours them orange (low pLDDT).

Transcription factors often combine a folded DNA-binding core with flexible tails that take partners, PTMs, and signalling into account.

FOXJ1 is a FOX-family forkhead factor. In the DNA-binding region, three helices pack as a bundle with two wing loops. Helix H3 sits in the DNA major groove; the wings contact the backbone. That layout is called a winged-helix fold.

Active site, the DNA-binding surface

FOXJ1 is not an enzyme, so it has no catalytic active site in the usual sense. The functional surface is the forkhead DNA-binding face: basic side chains (arginine, lysine, histidine) point toward the phosphates, and hydrophobic packing stabilises the helix bundle. CDD (Figure 6) flags this segment about residues 112 to 190. PDB 2C6Y shows a related forkhead domain on DNA (wwPDB [3]).

Post-translational modifications

FOXJ1 is regulated after translation like many transcription factors. Literature and proteomics report phosphorylation on FOXJ1 and related FOX proteins; kinase sites are common in the flexible tails. Full PTM lists are on UniProt Q92949, PTM/Processing [21].

Quaternary structure

FOXJ1 binds DNA as a monomer. It does not show a stable dimer interface like some enzymes. At promoters it still works together with other proteins (e.g. RFX3) in complexes that depend on cell context.

The forkhead domain in detail

The forkhead block (about 112 to 190, 79 residues) is the one structured domain in FOXJ1 and matches the FOX fold name. Three major helices and the two wings appear as helix plus short strand in JPred/PSIPRED (Figures 1 to 3), as blue/cyan in AlphaFold (Figure 5), as the dark block on PAE, and as the dense core in the Model 01 viewer.

The DNA-facing side is basic (arginine, lysine, histidine); the inside of the bundle is hydrophobic. Winged-helix proteins usually show that split.

Despite its small size, this domain does the DNA recognition. Longer N- and C-terminal regions handle nuclear import, activation domains, and regulation.

Transmembrane localization

No transmembrane segment is predicted for FOXJ1 in AlphaFold, JPred, or PSIPRED. UniProt lists it as soluble and nuclear. Import likely uses signals near the forkhead domain.
